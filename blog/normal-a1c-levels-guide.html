What Is a Normal A1C Level? A Complete Guide

7 min read

The three A1C ranges, at a glance

HbA1c (often just called A1C) measures the percentage of your hemoglobin that has glucose attached to it. Because red blood cells live for about three months, this single number gives a rolling average of your blood sugar over that period, rather than a snapshot like a finger-prick reading.

A1C resultCategoryEstimated average glucose
Below 5.7%NormalBelow ~117 mg/dL
5.7% – 6.4%Prediabetes~117–137 mg/dL
6.5% and aboveDiabetes140 mg/dL and above

These thresholds come from American Diabetes Association guidelines and are the ones most labs and clinics use in the US. Some countries report A1C in mmol/mol instead of a percentage, and international bodies occasionally set slightly different cutoffs, so it's worth checking which scale your lab report uses.

What counts as a "good" A1C if you already have diabetes

If you've been diagnosed with diabetes, "normal" isn't usually the target your doctor gives you — a personalized goal is. Many adults are told to aim for below 7%, but that number can be adjusted higher or lower depending on age, how long you've had diabetes, other health conditions, and your risk of low blood sugar episodes. A target that's right for someone else may not be right for you, which is why this is a conversation to have directly with whoever manages your care.

What can throw off an A1C result

A1C is reliable, but it isn't perfect. A handful of things can push your number up or down without actually reflecting your average glucose:

  • Anemia and blood loss — conditions that affect red blood cell lifespan can skew results in either direction.
  • Certain hemoglobin variants (like sickle cell trait) can make lab results inaccurate depending on the testing method used.
  • Kidney disease and liver disease can both affect A1C independently of blood sugar.
  • Pregnancy changes red blood cell turnover, so A1C is generally not used to diagnose diabetes during pregnancy.
  • Recent blood transfusions can temporarily distort the result.

If your A1C doesn't match what your home glucose readings suggest, this is often why — and it's a good reason to mention any of the above to your doctor.

Worth knowing: A1C reflects an average — it can't tell you about swings between highs and lows. Two people with the same A1C can have very different day-to-day glucose patterns, which is one reason doctors sometimes pair it with continuous glucose monitoring data.

How often should you get it checked

For most adults without diabetes, A1C isn't tested routinely unless there's a specific risk factor — being overweight, a family history of diabetes, or symptoms like increased thirst or fatigue. If you're in the prediabetes range, doctors typically recommend retesting once a year. If you have diabetes, testing every 3 to 6 months is common, since that roughly matches how long it takes the number to fully reflect a change in your management.

The bottom line

A1C is one of the most useful long-term markers for blood sugar health precisely because it can't be skewed by what you ate the morning of the test. But it's one data point, not the whole picture — it works best alongside a conversation with a doctor who knows your history.
